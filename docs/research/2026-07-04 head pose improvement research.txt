  TL;DR

  The "head movement compensation" in WebEyeTrack is not geometric — head pose
  is merely 6 numbers (3-dim head vector + 3-dim face origin) concatenated with
  512 image features and fed into a tiny 16→16→2 MLP that directly regresses
  screen coordinates. Whatever compensation exists must be learned, and
  calibration actively destroys it: the demo fine-tunes that whole MLP plus fits
  an affine correction using samples taken at one single head pose, so the
  correction is only valid in that posture. The bottom-edge bias has several
  compounding causes, including a hard-coded clamp in the demo that makes the
  bottom 5% of the screen literally unreachable, calibration points that never
  go below ±0.4, and blink-detection snapping half-closed (downward-gazing) eyes
  to screen center. The paper (arXiv:2508.19544) never demonstrates head-pose
  invariance — no ablation, no vertical-vs-horizontal error breakdown — and a
  GitHub issue independently confirms calibration-induced degradation.

  How the pipeline actually works

  Frame → MediaPipe FaceLandmarker (478 landmarks + facial transformation
  matrix) → a 128×512 eye-strip crop → BlazeGaze CNN encoder (frozen) →
  flattened 512-dim embedding → concatenated with head_vector and face_origin_3d
  → Dense(16)→Dense(16)→Dense(2) (blazegaze.py:213-217) → normalized screen
  point, centered at 0 with range [-0.5, 0.5] → optional affine transform →
  Kalman filter.

  Notably, model_based.py:401-939 contains a complete geometric gaze pipeline —
  eyeball-sphere ray estimation, head-pose rotation composition, and
  gaze-ray/screen-plane intersection (compute_pog, screen_plane_intersection) —
  but it is dead code. Nothing in the live path ever calls it. The shipped
  product relies entirely on the MLP implicitly learning head compensation.

  Why the gaze follows your head (ranked causes)

  1. Calibration overfits to your calibration-time head pose. adapt()
  (webeyetrack.py:320-428) runs 10 Adam steps (demo: lr=1e-4, main.py:245-252)
  on the entire gaze MLP using samples where head_vector and face_origin_3d are
  nearly constant. With constant pose inputs, the fine-tune freely co-adapts the
  pose-feature weights to act as a bias term. Simultaneously a 2×3 affine map
  is least-squares-fitted from just 4 points — also pose-specific. When you then
  turn or tilt your head, the eye-patch appearance changes (the strip is
  cropped by face-aligned homography, so a rotated head yields a
  different-looking patch), and neither the overfitted MLP nor the affine has
  any valid correction for it — the prediction drifts with the head. This
  matches the gaze-decomposition literature: few-shot personalization learns a
  bias valid only at the calibration pose (Chen & Shi, arXiv:1905.04451).
  2. The pose features the MLP could use are noisy and inconsistent. The metric
  scale hangs entirely on a hard-coded 1.2 cm iris diameter, estimated once from
  the first frame and frozen (webeyetrack.py:247-248). Two mutually
  inconsistent camera models are used in the same reconstruction: a fixed 60°
  vertical FOV perspective matrix (constants.py:65) and a fallback intrinsics
  guess of focal length = image width (utilities.py:399-433, called without FOV
  at webeyetrack.py:251). And the Euler-angle sign conventions differ between
  get_head_vector (model_based.py:82) and face_reconstruction
  (model_based.py:275) with hand-tuned "flip"/"swap" comments — pitch (the
  vertical axis) is exactly where an inconsistency would hurt.
  3. Train/inference feature mismatch in the default model. The shipped weights
  are blazegaze_mpiifacegaze.keras (constants.py:9), and the MPIIFaceGaze
  preprocessing feeds the net the dataset's ground-truth 3D face center
  (scripts/preprocessing/mpiifacegaze.py:149,294) — not the output of the
  MediaPipe reconstruction used at inference. So the live face_origin_3d comes
  from a different distribution than the one the model was trained on, weakening
  whatever pose compensation was learned.
  4. Structural capacity. 6 pose numbers out of 518 inputs into a 16-unit
  bottleneck gives the network very little machinery to disentangle pose from
  gaze in the first place, and the training sets (MPIIFaceGaze/GazeCapture) have
  limited head-pose diversity — the very motivation for ETH-XGaze
  (arXiv:2007.15837).

  Why the bottom edge reads too high (ranked causes)

  1. The demo clamps y to ±0.45 (main.py:377-380) while the screen spans ±0.5 —
  the bottom (and top) ~5% of the screen is unreachable by construction,
  independent of model quality.
  2. Calibration never samples the bottom. Only 4 corner points at (±0.4, ±0.4)
  (calibration_widget.py:20-25) — no center, no edge midpoints. The 6-parameter
  affine is fitted on exactly 4 points (the gate is len(support_y) > 3,
  webeyetrack.py:383) with zero redundancy, and everything below y=0.4 is
  extrapolation. Any compression of the model's vertical range there goes
  uncorrected.
  3. Blink handling corrupts bottom calibration points. Looking down droops the
  eyelids; when EAR drops below 0.2 the library returns norm_pog = (0,0) — which
  in this centered coordinate system is the screen center, not a sentinel
  (webeyetrack.py:594-610). The demo appends these to the calibration buffer
  unfiltered (main.py:390-391), dragging the per-point mean (and possibly the
  selected "centermost" sample, main.py:216-237) toward screen center for the
  two bottom corners specifically.
  4. Structural vertical weakness. The eye patch is a 128×512 strip, so the
  encoder has ~4× less vertical than horizontal resolution; downward gaze
  partially occludes the iris behind the eyelid; and webcams sit above the
  screen, so downward gaze is foreshortened. Literature consistently reports
  vertical error ~1.5–2× horizontal for webcam gaze tracking — the paper never
  even reports the split.

  Paper and community findings

  The paper is "WebEyeTrack: Scalable Eye-Tracking for the Browser via On-Device
  Few-Shot Personalization", arXiv:2508.19544 (Davalos et al., Vanderbilt).
  Points relevant to your symptoms:

  - Head pose is handled exactly as the code shows: metric head pose recovered
  via iris-scale + "radial Procrustes", then fed as an input feature — the paper
  contains no ablation showing this actually cancels head motion, and no
  horizontal/vertical error breakdown.
  - The paper itself admits browser deployment lacks true camera intrinsics and
  that the 1.2 cm iris constant is a population average.
  - Its own cross-dataset test shows drift: 7.24 cm error after 9-point
  calibration degrading to 8.72 cm over a 20-minute session.
  - On the GitHub tracker, issue #6 independently reports that accumulating
  calibration clicks in one area degrades performance, and notes the JS library
  doesn't even implement the paper's MAML adaptation (it uses affine + one-step
  Adam). No maintainer response; the repo appears lightly maintained (an
  unaddressed TF.js memory-leak report sits in PR #7). Your two symptoms are not
  yet documented anywhere — no issue mentions "head pose" or "vertical".
  - No substantive discussion exists on HN/Reddit/blogs yet; the paper is from
  August 2025.

  What could actually be done about it

  In rough order of effort-to-payoff for the Python demo:

  1. Remove or fix the y-clamp in main.py:377-380 (make it symmetric ±0.5) —
  trivial.
  2. Filter gaze_state == 'closed' frames out of calibration collection at
  main.py:390-391, and consider lowering the EAR threshold since downward gaze
  naturally reduces EAR.
  3. Use more calibration points including screen edges/center (e.g. a 3×3 grid
  with a bottom-center point at y≈0.48) and feed more than one sample per point
  into the affine fit.
  4. Calibrate at multiple head poses — have the user repeat a few points with
  head turned left/right/up/down. That's the only way the current architecture
  (pose as learned feature) can pick up a pose-dependent correction;
  alternatively, freeze the pose-feature weights during adapt() and only
  fine-tune the bias/affine so calibration can't destroy the pretrained pose
  behavior.
  5. Longer-term: wire up the dead geometric pipeline (compute_pog /
  screen-plane intersection) as a pose-compensated prior, or at least reconcile
  the two camera models and the Euler-sign conventions.

  Worth knowing: your symptoms are consistent with structural limitations of the
  method as shipped, not a misconfiguration on your side — and nobody has filed
  them upstream yet, so a GitHub issue with these findings would likely be the
  first concrete report. I can draft one, or start on fixes 1–3 in the demo, if
  you'd like.

Second opinion and concrete direction (2026-07-04)

RollScore needs sufficiently accurate vertical gaze to identify the staff system
being read. Horizontal sweep detection and sequential constraints are useful,
but they do not remove this vertical-accuracy requirement.

Recommended component decision: fork and improve WebEyeTrack's browser
implementation. Do not invest the first development cycle in WebGazer.js, a
ground-up gaze estimator, or a research-model port. WebEyeTrack is imperfect,
but it already has the two inputs the solution needs: an eye-appearance
embedding and explicit head-pose/position features. WebGazer has no pose input;
the alternative wide-pose models generally lack browser-ready point-of-gaze and
screen-mapping support.

The first improvement should be a RollScore-specific dynamic multi-pose
calibration layer, not the dormant geometric ray pipeline or full-network
retraining. At several screen targets, the user should maintain fixation while
slowly moving through the intended operating envelope (initially about +/-10-15
degrees yaw, +/-8-10 degrees pitch, plus modest translation). Fit a small,
regularized pose-conditioned residual model on top of WebEyeTrack's raw output,
for example corrected_y as a function of raw_y, pitch, yaw, head position, and a
few raw_y-by-pose interactions. Keep the encoder frozen. A pooled low-capacity
model is preferable to per-pose affine buckets, which require spatial coverage
in every bucket and introduce interpolation boundaries.

The evidence supports a realistic target of roughly 20-30% lower overall error
under moderate pose variation. A 30-50% reduction may be possible specifically
for the smooth head-correlated part of the error, but should not be promised
before measurement. The BMVC 2025 MobilePoG study found that pose diversity in
calibration mattered more than adding screen points at one pose; representative
dynamic-vs-static improvements were about 20-30%. The earlier 30-50% estimate in
this note was therefore too confident as an overall-error estimate.

Before implementing the correction, run a fixed-target head-sweep diagnostic:
log raw gaze, target, head pose and position; train on part of the pose range;
and test on held-out pose sectors and a later session. Continue with WebEyeTrack
only if the error is substantially smooth, repeatable, and pose-correlated. If
eye occlusion or unstable embeddings dominate, replace the base estimator with
a wide-pose model while retaining the calibration and staff-classification
layers. Building and training a gaze model from scratch is the last resort.

Judge success by RollScore's actual task: correct staff-system classification,
adjacent-system confusion, held-out-pose performance, continuous-reading
stability, and cross-session repeatability. A provisional target is >=95%
correct system identification in the normal pose envelope, with nearly all
remaining errors limited to an adjacent system. The vertical p90 error must be
comfortably less than half the distance between neighboring system centerlines.
